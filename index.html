01 — Foundation

MINDSET & CORE TRUTHS

Before a single lap, you need the mental framework that separates drivers who progress from those who plateau. These are non-negotiable.

🧠TRUTH 01

Race Against Yourself First

At the grassroots level, your only real opponent is your previous self. Comparing your time to a veteran who's been on the same track for 5 years is meaningless. Track your progression event-over-event.

METRIC → LAP TIME DELTA BETWEEN SESSIONS
🔁TRUTH 02

Consistency Beats Speed

A driver who runs 10 clean laps within 0.2 seconds of each other is more coachable, safer, and more valuable to a team than one who blazes fast then crashes. Consistency is the whole game early on.

TARGET → SAME LINE, EVERY LAP
📷TRUTH 03

Data Is Your Coach

A GoPro on your windshield costs $0 in coaching if you review footage the same night. Where are you braking? Where are you looking? Film everything, review everything. This alone will cut seconds.

TOOL → GOPRO + HARRY'S LAPTIMER APP
👂TRUTH 04

Be Coachable, Not Defensive

Every instructor, faster driver, and corner worker knows something you don't. Kill your ego in the paddock. Ask dumb questions. Say "I don't know." Drivers who progress fastest treat every session like a school day.

HABIT → ONE TAKEAWAY WRITTEN DOWN PER SESSION
🛠️TRUTH 05

Learn the Car, Not Just Driving

Understanding weight transfer, tire heat cycles, brake fade, and oversteer vs understeer will change how you drive. The best grassroots drivers are also amateur engineers. Read "Speed Secrets" by Ross Bentley.

BOOK → SPEED SECRETS · ROSS BENTLEY
🤝TRUTH 06

The Paddock Is Your Network

The person who lends you tires, lets you co-drive their endurance car, refers you to a team — all of that starts in the paddock on a Tuesday night at Harris Hill. Show up consistently.

ACTION → MEET 3 NEW PEOPLE EVERY EVENT
On being a woman in motorsports right now: The grassroots and amateur racing world is actively welcoming female drivers. You will stand out — use that intentionally. Women in Motorsports North America (WIMNA), Path to the Pits, and NASCAR Drive for Diversity all have funding and mentorship programs specifically for you. The diversity push in motorsports is real, funded, and looking for people exactly like you.

03 — Organizations

SCCA VS NASA

The two major grassroots racing sanctioning bodies in the US. You don't pick one — you join both. $125/year total is the best investment you'll make.

🏁 SCCA — Sports Car Club of America

  • Founded 1944. Over 65,000 members nationally. The original American club.
  • Best known for Autocross (Solo), Road Racing, Rally. All three accessible to beginners.
  • Your local chapter: Lone Star Region SCCA — lonestarscca.org
  • Autocross requires NO license — show up and race same day. $40–60 entry.
  • Track Night in America is run under SCCA — beginner-friendly, non-competitive.
  • Club Racing School weekend earns your provisional competition license (~$300–400).
  • Spec MX-5 (your NC) and STR class live here. National championship: "The Runoffs."
  • Annual membership: ~$80/year. Weekend membership ~$20 if not a member.
  • SCCA ProSolo has a Ladies Challenge category — first women-specific competition.

🔵 NASA — National Auto Sport Association

  • Founded 1991. More progressive licensing structure. $45/year.
  • HPDE ladder (1–4) is the best structured beginner-to-racer pathway in US motorsports.
  • HPDE1–4: instructor in car at level 1, solo open-passing at level 4.
  • Time Trial license available after HPDE3 — no competition school required.
  • Competition license requires HPDE4 + Competition School weekend (~$400–800).
  • Team Endurance Racing Challenge (TREC) is NASA's budget endurance series.
  • Honda Challenge, Spec Miata, Camaro Spec, Super Touring classes in Texas.
  • FIA Bronze rating application flows through Motorsport Safety Foundation (NASA affiliated).
  • Texas region runs at Harris Hill and Motorsport Ranch Cresson — check drivenasa.com.
Bottom line: Join both. SCCA for autocross right now (no license, cheapest entry point in motorsport). NASA for HPDE progression and your eventual competition license. The two licenses cross-recognize each other for most events. FIA Bronze rating application flows through NASA's national structure.
04 — The Progression

THE FULL RACING LADDER

Every rung from absolute zero to wheel-to-wheel club racing. Not everyone goes all the way — but here's every step so you know exactly where you are and what's next.

Level 01 — Zero Experience
KARTING & TRACK NIGHT
Low-speed, high-feedback environments to confirm you love this. COTA Karting's 3-week beginner course and Track Night in America at Harris Hill (26 miles from Austin) are your two entry points. These aren't races — they're learning environments.
  • COTA Karting Course — 3-week program, ~$150. Learn the line. Focus vision ahead, not at the barrier.
  • Track Night @ Harris Hill — Your NC Miata, instructor in passenger seat, non-competitive, ~$150–200.
  • Corner Work at SCCA — Free. Volunteer at a Lone Star SCCA event. See racecraft from outside the car.
~$300 TOTAL1–3 MONTHSNO LICENSE NEEDED
Level 02 — Beginner
SCCA AUTOCROSS
Timed cone course in a parking lot. 5–6 runs per event. Your NC competes as your daily driver — no modifications needed. Max speed ~55mph. No license. Teaches precision, trail braking, car rotation, and vision better than anything else at this stage. Run minimum 6–8 events before moving on.
  • Lone Star Region SCCA Autocross — Join ($80/yr), show up, pay $40–60. NC Miata in STR or E-Street class.
  • Walk the course on foot first — Before every single run. Mental lap before physical lap, every event.
  • GoPro every run, review same night — Where are you lifting unnecessarily? Where are your hands jerky?
  • SCCA ProSolo — After 10+ events. Side-by-side runs, Ladies Challenge category.
$40–60/EVENTSPRING–FALL SEASONNO MODS NEEDED
Level 03 — Intermediate
NASA HPDE 1–4
The most structured, safest beginner-to-racer pathway in American motorsports. Progress through four levels with increasing skill requirements. At HPDE1 an instructor rides constantly. By HPDE4 you're solo in open-passing alongside licensed racers. No prescribed timeline — you advance when your instructor says you're ready.
  • HPDE1 — Instructor in car at all times. Flags, track limits, designated passing zones. Safety and awareness first.
  • HPDE2 — Instructor may allow solo laps. Passing still in designated zones only.
  • HPDE3 — Point-by passing allowed anywhere. Instructor optional. Technique starts clicking here. Time Trial license eligible.
  • HPDE4 — Solo, open passing, racing pace. Driving alongside licensed racers. Prerequisite for Competition School.
$200–400/EVENT4–12 MONTHSNASA MEMBERSHIP REQUIRED
Level 04 — Licensed Competitor
TIME TRIALS & COMPETITION SCHOOL
After HPDE3: apply for your NASA Time Trial license — competitive lap timing, officially competitive. After HPDE4: attend Competition School and earn your provisional racing license for wheel-to-wheel combat.
  • NASA Competition School — Weekend intensive, ~$400–800. Race starts, side-by-side, flags, CCR rulebook. Provisional license earned.
  • SCCA Competition School — Regional weekend equivalent, ~$300–500. Can use same car as your HPDE car.
$400–800 SCHOOL6–18 MONTHS INPROVISIONAL LICENSE EARNED
Level 05 — Wheel to Wheel
CLUB RACING — SCCA / NASA REGIONAL
You are now a licensed racing driver. 20–40 minute sprint races, door-to-door, grid starts, real flags. Spec MX-5, STR, or IT classes for your NC. Provisional license upgrades to full after 4–8 races without incident.
  • Lone Star SCCA Regionals — Sprint racing at Harris Hill and MSR Cresson. Spec MX-5 or STR class for your NC.
  • NASA Texas Club Racing — Same format. Honda Challenge, Spec Miata, Super Touring also run here.
  • Borrow friends' cars — BRZ, S2000, Golf R — cover fuel + entry, run a session in a different car.
$500–1,200/WEEKENDONGOINGFULL LICENSE AFTER 4–8 RACES
Level 06 — Endurance Racing
LEMONS / CHAMPCAR / AER / WRL
Team-based 7–24hr races. Join someone else's team, get 4–8 hours of seat time in a completely different car for $500–1,500. This is the fastest path to FIA Bronze rating and GT3 team relationships.
  • 24 Hours of Lemons — No license required. $500–800/seat. Most fun per dollar in motorsport.
  • ChampCar Endurance — Slightly more serious. Point-based VPI system. Ran at Harris Hill.
  • American Endurance Racing (AER) — Time-based classing. Preferred experience. Excellent cars.
  • World Racing League (WRL) — Power-to-weight classing. Requires license or HPDE experience.
$500–1,500/SEATANY CAR, ANY TIME AFTER HPDEBUILDS FIA RATING HOURS

06 — Execution

YEAR-BY-YEAR PLAN

Specific, actionable phases. Not theory — real things you do in real order.

01
TASTE THE SPORT — MONTHS 1–2
Jan – Feb 2026
Zero pressure. You are a sponge. Show up, feel it, decide you want more.
  • Step 1 → Join Lone Star Region SCCA ($80) and NASA ($45).
  • Step 2 → Attend a Lone Star SCCA autocross as a corner worker/volunteer. Learn the rules, meet everyone, spend $0.
  • Step 3 → COTA Karting 3-week beginner course.
  • Step 4 → Buy your Snell SA2020 helmet in person.
  • Step 5 → Set up sim: Logitech G29 + Assetto Corsa. Practice the Harris Hill layout before you drive it.
02
FIRST AUTOCROSS SEASON — MONTHS 3–6
Mar – Jun 2026
Your NC Miata is your weapon. First competition. Drive your own car, learn your own car.
  • Step 1 → Enter your first SCCA autocross. Drive E-Street or STR class. Walk the course twice on foot before you drive it.
  • Step 2 → Run 4–6 autocross events this season. Track time improvement event-to-event.
  • Step 3 → Mount GoPro. Review every run same evening.
  • Step 4 → Ask a faster driver in your class if you can ride along. Worth 10 solo sessions.
  • Step 5 → Read "Speed Secrets" by Ross Bentley. Do it now, not later.
03
FIRST TRACK DAYS — MONTHS 4–7
Apr – Jul 2026
Your NC takes its first real track laps. Autocross knowledge meets real-circuit driving.
  • Step 1 → Pre-track checklist: fresh brake pads, Motul RBF600 flush, tire tread check, torque lug nuts. Remove all loose items.
  • Step 2 → Register for Track Night in America at Harris Hill. Instructor in passenger seat, ~$150–200.
  • Step 3 → Register for a full NASA HPDE event. Start in HPDE1. Goal: memorize flags, corners, passing zones — not lap time.
  • Step 4 → Target 2–3 track days this summer, progressing through HPDE1 into HPDE2.
  • Step 5 → Propose a track-day trade with a friend who has a BRZ, S2000, or Golf R.
04
HPDE PROGRESSION + FIRST ENDURANCE — MONTHS 7–10
Jul – Oct 2026
Advancing through HPDE2 into HPDE3. Your first endurance race — in someone else's car.
  • Step 1 → When cleared for HPDE3, apply for NASA Time Trial license — your first official competition credential.
  • Step 2 → Join a Lemons or ChampCar team as a co-driver. Pay seat fee ($500–800), help prep the car, get into a different car.
  • Step 3 → Upload GoPro footage to Blayze.io for a paid coaching session with a professional driver.
05
GET LICENSED — MONTHS 11–14
Nov 2026 – Feb 2027
Competition School. Provisional license. You are now officially a race car driver.
  • Step 1 → Complete HPDE4. Final prerequisite for Competition School.
  • Step 2 → Register for NASA Competition School (Texas region). Pass written CCR test + on-track evaluation. ~$400–800.
  • Step 3 → Receive NASA Provisional Competition License.
  • Step 4 → Install Hard Dog or Bethania Garage roll bar on your NC (~$1,000 installed).
06
FIRST RACING SEASON — 2027
Spring – Fall 2027
First full season of wheel-to-wheel. Goal: finishing, learning, building your resume.
  • Step 1 → Enter your first SCCA or NASA regional race. Goal: clean first lap, no contact, finish the race.
  • Step 2 → Target 4–6 regional races. Provisional license upgrades to full after 4–8 clean races.
  • Step 3 → Run 1–2 endurance events with a mixed team. Drive other cars, build team chemistry.
  • Step 4 → Start your racing resume document. Every event, car, class, result — logged from day one.
09 — The Paperwork

LICENSING PATHWAYS

Every license you can earn and exactly how. Understand this early — it maps directly to your progression timeline and your eventual FIA Bronze rating.

🔵 NASA Pathway

  • Join NASA ($45/yr) at drivenasa.com
  • HPDE1 — instructor always present, any safe street car
  • HPDE2 — solo laps begin, designated passing zones
  • HPDE3 — point-by passing, Time Trial license eligible
  • Apply for NASA TT License — competitive lap timing, no school needed
  • HPDE4 — open passing, race pace, Comp School prerequisite
  • Attend NASA Competition School (~$400–800)
  • Pass written CCR test + on-track evaluation
  • Receive NASA Provisional Competition License
  • Complete 4–8 clean races → full National License
  • Apply for FIA Bronze via Motorsport Safety Foundation

🏁 SCCA Pathway

  • Join SCCA ($80/yr) at scca.com
  • Autocross — no license, enters same day, builds reputation
  • Track Night in America events — SCCA-run non-competitive HPDE
  • SCCA Time Trials event → Time Trials license
  • SCCA Club Racing School weekend, Lone Star Region (~$300–500)
  • Pass on-track evaluation + written GCR exam
  • SCCA Regional Competition License (Provisional)
  • 4 clean regional races → full National License
  • National License + results → eligible for Majors events
  • Majors history → eligible for The Runoffs (national championship)
Cross-licensing: NASA and SCCA generally recognize each other's licenses for cross-entering events. Lemons and ChampCar experience counts as documented track time for both licensing applications. Your FIA Bronze rating (earned through NASA) is the credential that opens GT4 and GT3 doors internationally.

GT3 — The Goal

PATH TO GT3

GT3 racing at the IMSA level is the most prestigious accessible motorsport tier in the world. The GTD class mandates one FIA Bronze or Silver amateur driver per entry. That seat is your target.

What is IMSA GTD? The IMSA WeatherTech Championship GTD class uses GT3-spec cars — Porsche 911 GT3 R, Ferrari 296 GT3, Lamborghini Huracán GT3 Evo2, BMW M4 GT3, Aston Martin Vantage GT3, Mercedes-AMG GT3, Corvette Z06 GT3.R — and requires each car to include at least one FIA Bronze or Silver amateur driver in a Pro-Am pairing. This structure is specifically designed to make world-class GT3 cars accessible to funded, skilled, FIA-rated amateur drivers. You are the Am in this equation.
01

Club Racing License + Regional Wins

Earn your SCCA or NASA competition license. Run 6–10 clean regional races in Spec MX-5 or STR class. Zero incidents is your metric, not lap times. Start your racing resume on day one — log every event, every car, every instructor. This is the document you show teams.

2026NASA/SCCA LICENSE6–10 CLEAN RACES
02

FIA Bronze Rating + 20+ Endurance Hours

The FIA driver rating system (Bronze → Silver → Gold → Platinum) determines your GTD eligibility. Bronze is what you need. Apply through the Motorsport Safety Foundation with documented club racing experience and endurance racing hours. AER and WRL sessions count toward FIA rating applications.

2027FIA BRONZEMOTORSPORT SAFETY FOUNDATION
03

GT4 — Porsche Sprint Challenge or SRO GT4 America

GT4 is the direct feeder class for GT3. Porsche Sprint Challenge North America runs alongside IMSA at Daytona, Sebring, Long Beach, Miami, and Austin — you're in the IMSA paddock, visible to GT3 team principals, while racing. Budget: $150K–$300K/year. Your content brand + automotive sponsors + WIMNA scholarships are all funding levers here.

2028$150K–300K/YEARPORSCHE SPRINT CHALLENGE

GT3 Co-Drive — IMSA GTD Pro-Am

The Pro-Am seat. You bring your FIA Bronze/Silver rating, your budget ($200K–$500K for a full season, $150K–$300K for a single endurance entry), and your driving record. Teams like Wright Motorsports (Porsche), Pfaff Motorsports (Lamborghini), Triarsi Competizione (Ferrari), Turner Motorsport (BMW), and Heart of Racing (Aston) all field GTD entries built for amateur co-drivers. First target: a single Rolex 24 at Daytona GTD entry.

The Driver

DRIVER TECHNIQUE & CRAFT

What separates amateur GT3 drivers who get second seasons from those who don't isn't raw speed. It's precision communication, mental performance, and the ability to maintain consistency when the car is degrading at 2AM during a 24-hour race.

🎯TECHNIQUE 01

Trail Braking

Carrying brake pressure past the turn-in point, gradually releasing through initial corner phase. This loads the front tires for maximum grip during turn-in. Almost no one does this correctly at club level. Learn it on sim first, then at HPDE3+ where you have room to experiment safely.

PRACTICE TOOL → IRACING / ASSETTO CORSA FIRST
👁️TECHNIQUE 02

Vision — Look Where You Go

Your eyes must always be looking at the next point on track — not the apex you're at, but the next corner's apex. In a GT3 car at 150mph, your eyes need to be 3–4 seconds ahead. This is a trainable habit. Start practicing it in street driving right now.

DRILL → LOOK 30% FURTHER AHEAD THAN FEELS NATURAL
🧠TECHNIQUE 03

The 2AM Stint

Endurance racing includes night stints — the 2–4AM window. You're sleep-deprived, the car runs differently in cold air, traffic is chaotic in the dark. Mental training is not optional: visualization, box breathing, focus drills, and cold exposure training all build the resilience for these moments.

🚥TECHNIQUE 04

The Art of the Pass

A clean pass that costs you 0.3 seconds beats an aggressive pass that puts you both in the wall. In GT3 traffic you have to out-brake someone into a corner while managing tire temps, their defensive line, and your ability to make the apex. Patience is racecraft. Develop it in endurance racing.

🔊TECHNIQUE 05

Debriefing Your Engineer

"It felt a bit off" is useless. "The car understeers in the first third of Turn 7 on entry" is what engineers need — technical, precise, reproducible feedback. This is a learnable skill. Practice it in every HPDE debrief starting now, even when the feedback is basic.

💪TECHNIQUE 06

Physical Demands of GT3

GT3 cars generate 3–4G braking, 2–3G cornering. Your head + helmet = 6–7kg. At 3G braking, your neck supports 20kg of lateral load. Over a 3-hour Sebring stint in 95°F heat you'll lose 3–4kg in sweat. Your strength training and neck work aren't vanity — they're specifically preparing you for this.

PRIORITY → NECK STRENGTH, GRIP ENDURANCE, HEAT TOLERANCE

🎯

The Pan Shot — Core Technique

  • Shutter speed: Start at 1/200s for cars at 60–80mph. Drop to 1/80–1/125s as you improve.
  • Technique: Follow the car from the moment it enters your frame, fire burst as it passes, continue following through. Never stop mid-pan.
  • Position: Perpendicular to car's path, slight elevation above track level. Corners give more dwell time than straights.
  • Burst mode: 5–8 frames. One sharp frame is a win. Practice at SCCA autocross first.

08 — The Simulator

YOUR SIM SETUP

The sim is not optional anymore. Every competitive driver at every level uses simulation. Here's exactly what to buy, what to practice, and what the sim genuinely cannot replace.

🎮 Budget Starter (~$300)

  • Logitech G29 or G923 wheel + pedals — buy used on Facebook Marketplace, $150–200
  • Wheelstand Pro or Playseat Challenge ($80–120) — mounts to any chair
  • Assetto Corsa (PC) — $20 on Steam. Has Harris Hill, COTA, and most tracks you'll drive
  • Gran Turismo 7 (PS5) — if you have a PS5, this is turnkey and excellent for track learning
TOTAL: ~$250–400

🔧 Mid-Tier (~$800–1,200)

  • Thrustmaster T300RS or Fanatec CSL DD (direct drive) — massive force feedback improvement
  • Thrustmaster T-LCM load cell pedals — the brake pedal changes everything
  • GT Omega or Next Level Racing rig frame
  • iRacing subscription ($13/month) — the gold standard. GT3 cars are in iRacing.
TOTAL: ~$800–1,200

📚 What to Practice

  • Learn track layouts before every real event — memorize braking markers, apex points for Harris Hill, COTA, MSR Cresson
  • Trail braking — carrying brake pressure into corners the sim teaches safely
  • Race starts in iRacing — the first lap is the most dangerous; sim gives you infinite repetitions
  • Consistency drills — try to run 10 laps within 0.3 seconds of each other. That skill transfers directly.

⚠️ Sim Limitations

  • Sim does NOT replicate G-forces, heat, vibration, or physical fatigue accurately
  • Sim does NOT replace seat time — treat it like film study, not practice
  • Bad habits from sim CAN transfer — over-aggressive inputs, lack of spatial awareness
  • Use sim to learn tracks and technique concepts; real-world seat time is irreplaceable

Racing drivers are athletes. Your kinesiology background puts you miles ahead of most beginners — now apply it to the specific demands of the car. GT3 cars generate up to 4G. Your heart rate averages 170+ bpm across an endurance race.

  • Farmer carries 3×40 yards

  • Easy 20–30 min run at conversational pace
  • Yoga or mobility work
  • Foam roll hips, thoracic spine, hamstrings
  • Run Club day — community + movement

🔄 Friday — Race Simulation Circuit

4 rounds, minimal rest — simulates sustained race effort:

  • 10 box jumps
  • 10 DB rows
  • 30 sec plank
  • 10 goblet squats
  • 250m row or 1 min ski erg
  • 10 lateral med ball slams

🚴 Saturday — Long Cardio

  • 45–60 min bike, run, or row at conversational pace
  • Do some of this outside in summer — heat tolerance is a real racing performance factor
  • A driver who overheats before the car does is leaving lap time on the table
Race Day Nutrition

🥗 Daily Baseline

  • Protein: 0.8–1g per lb bodyweight — sustain grip endurance and postural strength all race
  • Carbs: moderate-high on training days. Your brain runs on glucose and cognitive load in a race is enormous.
  • Fats: anti-inflammatory priority — avocado, olive oil, salmon, walnuts
  • Hydration: 80–100oz water daily minimum. Dehydration tanks reaction time before you feel thirsty.
  • Your chef background is your secret weapon — you're already building the right meals for clients

🏁 Pre-Track Day Protocol

  • Night before: complex carb-forward — rice, sweet potatoes, lean protein, veggies. No heavy dairy.
  • Morning of: oatmeal + banana + PB, or eggs + toast. Eat 2–2.5 hours before you strap in.
  • Hydration: start drinking water the night before. 16oz before you leave the house.
  • Between sessions: electrolyte drink, banana, rice cakes. Avoid caffeine bombs.
  • Race day: same as above. No experiments. Nothing new. Simple, known foods only.
The racing fitness priority stack: (1) Neck strength — GT3 lateral loads are 20kg+ on your head. (2) Grip and forearm endurance — sustained steering input over 3-hour stints. (3) Cardiovascular base — heat + sustained cognitive load drain you faster than you think. (4) Core stability — keeps your torso braced through high-G corners. Your existing strength training baseline already addresses most of this. The specific additions are neck work and heat exposure training.
